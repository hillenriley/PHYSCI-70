This was a completely new machine to me, but with Kassia and Suvin's explanations, it became pretty simple. All we had to do was connect one prong to the middle wire, and the other prong to the grounding wire, and read the values shown on the screen. </p> <p> This is what mine looked like when I read my values: </p> ![image of oscilloscope](./oscope.jpeg) ![image of oscilloscope](./oscope2.jpeg) ![image of oscilloscope](./oscope3.jpeg) ![image of oscilloscope](./oscope4.jpeg) <p> Reading the oscilloscope was slightly harder. This is how I was able to answer the proposed questions from the assignment: </p> <p> Is it on a fixed clock? The time domain for this machine showed 20.00μs, which I believe is relating back to the force of friction. I took physics this year and the symbol "μ" is usually representative of the force of friction value. I do not believe this oscilloscope is on a fixed clock because of the fact that we can change all of the values with the buttons and knobs on the side of the machine, but I could be wrong.</p> <p> What's its speed? The oscilloscope presented that there was 19.5159 Hz, which I am pretty sure is speed. Herz measures how many cycles an object goes through in one second, so this value would be the closest measured value to speed. </p> Here is a video of how my whole project turned out in the end: ![video of output project](./outputproj.mp4) <p> The last part of this assignment was to create Fusion (or in my case OnShape) designs for next weeks CNC cutting lesson.
